Work in progress and working papers

The Impact of Hospital-Physician Acquisitions on Patient Referrals
Nearly 30% of all physicians are financially integrated with a hospital (AMA, 2017). I examine the effect of hospital-physician acquisition on physician referral choice using the universe of Medicare claim-level data from 2007-2012. First, I document trends in physician referrals to the hospital employer. I find that hospital-acquired physicians are nine times as likely to refer to the owning hospital post-acquisition. Physicians with larger referral volumes are also more likely to be hospital acquired. Next, I use panel difference-in-difference methods along with a Coarsened Exact Weighting algorithm to show that physicians are 7 to 10 percentage points more likely to refer to the owning hospital for the first two years post-acquisition. Physicians whose practices are divested from a hospital are 6 to 8 percentage points less likely to refer to their previous employer.

The Impact of Medical Malpractice Laws on Ob-Gyn Entry
The state of Texas implemented a punitive damage cap reduction of 50% along with other administrative changes that increase patients' costs of claiming malpractice in September 2003. In this paper, I examine ob-gyn entry and location patterns across Texas counties using medical licensing information from the Texas Medical Board for 2000 to 2008. First, I document a 10% decrease in medical malpractice premiums for ob-gyn specialties across the state immediately after the 2003 tort reform. This leads to a 5% increase in ob-gyn entry. Second, I estimate an entry model to show that the increased ob-gyn entry is concentrated in the least populated counties. I find the probability that the most isolated areas have an ob-gyn increases by 55% immediately following the reform, and that the population required to sustain up to the first three ob-gyn practices is lower in these areas relative to more highly populated counties.
